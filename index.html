Infants with cleft lip and palate had a significantly higher prevalence of malnutrition than those with isolated cleft lip — 56% vs 30% respectively — with more severe cleft phenotypes posing greater feeding challenges.

Among 855 children undergoing initial cleft repair, 22.3% were at least moderately chronically malnourished at the time of surgery — establishing malnutrition as a measurable surgical risk factor.

A cross-sectional study found 68% of infants with CL±P were malnourished, with 57% experiencing moderate-to-severe malnutrition prior to surgical repair.

Tungotyo et al., BMC Pediatrics (2017)

Nutritional Stakes for Your Patients

"For many families, feeding becomes the first major medical challenge after diagnosis."

Surgical Readiness

  • Poor growth delays lip & palate repair
  • Weight thresholds required for anesthesia
  • Malnutrition impairs wound healing

Feeding Physiology

  • Increased energy expenditure from feeding effort
  • Feeding fatigue limits intake
  • Aspiration and airway risk

Family Impact

  • Parent stress and anxiety
  • Caregiver exhaustion
  • Bonding and breastfeeding challenges
★

Feeding challenges in these patients require a special focus on feeding ability and growth from all clinical staff involved. Early education to families and timely consultation with Speech-Language Pathology and Registered Dietitians are essential to optimizing outcomes.

Section 2
Normal Feeding Physiology vs. Cleft Feeding
Understanding the biomechanics of infant feeding and what changes with cleft anomalies.
Section 2 · Feeding Physiology
Normal Suck-Swallow-Breathe Coordination
1
Lip & Jaw Seal

Infant creates an airtight seal around nipple, enabling negative pressure generation.

2
Negative Intraoral Pressure

Tongue and soft palate work together to create suction, drawing milk into the oral cavity.

3
Peristaltic Tongue Motion

Anterior-to-posterior wave moves bolus toward pharynx.

4
Swallow-Breathe Coordination

1 suck : 1 swallow : 1 breath ratio in term infants — any disruption cascades quickly.

The Critical Factor

Normal feeding depends entirely on the ability to generate negative intraoral pressure — not possible with an open palate.

Normal Feeding Rate

  • ~1 oz per 10 minutes
  • Complete feed in 20–30 min
  • No excessive fatigue or respiratory effort
  • Consistent weight gain expected
Section 2 · Feeding Physiology
How Cleft Type Affects Feeding

Cleft Lip Only

  • Lip seal impaired
  • Suction generally preserved
  • Air leakage → aerophagia
  • Least feeding complexity

Cleft Palate

  • Cannot generate negative pressure
  • Nasal regurgitation common
  • Prolonged, exhausting feeds
  • Significant growth faltering risk

Pierre Robin Sequence

  • Micrognathia → tongue base obstruction
  • Glossoptosis; airway compromise
  • High NG/G-tube dependence rate
  • Most complex feeding challenge

Aerophagia

Air swallowing → abdominal distension, early satiety, and poor volume intake. Pacing and positioning significantly reduce this.

Nasal Regurgitation

Milk passes through the open palate into the nasal cavity — indicates poor seal, feed inefficiency, and aspiration risk if combined with airway compromise.

Common Thread

Across all craniofacial conditions, the shared challenge is inadequate oral intake relative to energy needs. The mechanism differs; the nutritional approach is often similar.

Visual Tips for Residents

  • Watch the infant's neck and chest during feeding
  • Nasal flaring = respiratory compromise
  • Color changes = stop feeding immediately
  • Noisy, wet breathing = possible aspiration

Section 3
Feeding Assessment
What to ask, what to observe, and when to escalate care.
Section 3 · Feeding Assessment
5 Questions Every Resident Should Ask
1
How long does each feed take?

Normal: 20–30 min. Over 30–40 min = feeding difficulty requiring evaluation.

2
Any coughing, choking, or color change?

Respiratory symptoms during feeds suggest aspiration until proven otherwise.

3
How much is the baby taking per feed and per day?

Track oz/kg/day. Inadequate volume often precedes weight faltering by days.

4
How is the weight gain trending?

Plot on growth chart every visit. Declining trajectory = nutrition intervention needed now.

5
How is the caregiver coping?

Caregiver burnout directly affects feeding consistency and infant outcomes.

Output to Monitor

  • Adequate wet diapers (≥6/day newborns)
  • Stool frequency and consistency
  • Signs of dehydration

Red Flags — Escalate Now

  • Feeds consistently >30–40 minutes
  • Poor or absent weight gain
  • Oxygen desaturations during feeding
  • Recurrent pneumonia
  • Clinical dehydration
★

When in doubt, refer to SLP for formal feeding evaluation. Consider MBSS if aspiration is suspected.

Section 3 · Feeding Assessment
Clinical Observation: What to Look For

Observe During a Feed

  • Positioning of infant and caregiver
  • Lip and jaw seal quality
  • Suck-swallow-breathe coordination
  • Respiratory effort (nasal flaring, retractions)
  • Nasal regurgitation occurrence
  • Feeding pacing and pauses
  • Signs of fatigue (decreased effort, drowsiness)

Stooling & Output

  • Adequate wet diapers (≥6/day for newborns)
  • Stool frequency and consistency
  • Signs of dehydration

Red Flags — Escalate Now

  • Feeds consistently >30–40 minutes
  • Poor or absent weight gain
  • Oxygen desaturations during feeding
  • Recurrent pneumonia or respiratory illness
  • Clinical dehydration
  • Caregiver unable to maintain feeding schedule
★

When in doubt, refer to SLP for formal feeding evaluation and consider MBSS if aspiration is suspected.

Section 4
Feeding Interventions
Specialty systems, positioning, techniques, and enteral support options.
Section 4 · Feeding Interventions
Specialty Feeders, Positioning & Techniques
FeederBest For
Haberman / Medela SpecialNeedsCleft palate, Pierre Robin, hypotonia — no suction needed
Pigeon NippleModerate cleft palate; one-way valve, minimal suction
Dr. Brown's SpecialtyCleft lip, mild CP; reduces aerophagia
Mead Johnson NurserSqueeze bottle; caregiver controls flow rate

Positioning & Support

  • Upright (45–90°) — reduces nasal regurgitation and aspiration
  • Side-lying appropriate for some cleft lip cases
  • Avoid supine — increases aspiration risk significantly
  • Chin and cheek support to improve seal

Pacing, Thickening & Cues

  • Pause every 3–5 sucks; tilt bottle down to reduce flow
  • Follow infant hunger/satiety cues, not the clock
  • Feed thickening only with confirmed aspiration + MBSS guidance
  • Risk of NEC with thickening in premature infants
★

No single feeder is universally superior. Trial with the family alongside SLP and dietitian guidance is the standard approach.

Section 4 · Feeding Interventions
Enteral Nutrition Support

Nasogastric (NG) Tube

  • Short-term bridge when oral feeds insufficient
  • Preserves opportunity for oral feeding practice
  • Commonly used in Pierre Robin sequence
  • Caregiver training required

Gastrostomy Tube (G-Tube)

  • Indicated for long-term inability to meet needs orally
  • Complex syndromic patients or persistent aspiration
  • Allows full nutrition without feeding fatigue
  • Surgical placement; site care required

Indications for Enteral Support

  • Unable to meet ≥75–80% of needs orally
  • Recurrent aspiration with oral feeds
  • Failure to thrive despite interventions
  • Pre-op nutritional optimization needed
  • Caregiver unable to sustain oral feeding demands
★

Enteral feeding is not a failure of oral feeding — it is a clinical tool to support growth while the infant develops oral skills and awaits surgical repair.

Growth & Nutrition Management
Energy targets, formula strategies, monitoring, and failure to thrive prevention.
Section 5 · Growth & Nutrition Management
Nutrition Goals & Targets
1.1–1.4x
EER — estimated energy requirement for catch-up growth
↑ Protein
Increased protein needs relative to standard recommendations

When to Increase Caloric Density

  • Volume limited by feed tolerance or fatigue
  • Growth velocity below expected
  • Approaching surgical weight threshold
  • Caregiver unable to sustain required feed volumes

Caloric Fortification Strategies

  • Concentrate formula — e.g., 22–24–27 kcal/oz
  • Add fat source — MCT oil, vegetable oil
  • Human milk fortifier — for breastmilk-fed infants
  • Modular carbohydrate — Duocal, Polycose

Hydration Goals

  • 100–150 mL/kg/day for neonates
  • Monitor wet diapers closely
  • At-risk for dehydration with prolonged feeds
Section 5 · Growth & Nutrition Management
Common Nutrition Management Mistakes

Mistake 1: Volume Before Density

Recommending increased feed volume when the infant is already fatigued. Increasing caloric density should come before pushing more volume.

Mistake 2: Ignoring Reflux

GERD is highly prevalent in cleft infants. Unmanaged reflux limits caloric retention and worsens intake and weight trends.

Mistake 3: "Wait Until After Surgery"

Assuming nutrition will improve automatically post-op. Surgery requires adequate growth — nutrition must happen first.

Mistake 4: Prolonged Exhausting Feeds

Feeds >40 minutes burn more calories than the infant ingests. Energy expenditure during a prolonged feed can negate its caloric value.

Growth Chart Monitoring

  • Plot weight, length, and HC at every visit
  • Track weight velocity — trends matter more than single points
  • Crossing ≥2 percentile lines downward = immediate action
★

When growth faltering is identified, consult dietitian and SLP as first response when available — before escalating to enteral support.

Section 6
Surgical Timeline & Nutrition Considerations
Pre- and post-operative nutritional guidance across the cleft repair journey.
Section 6 · Surgical Timeline
Surgical Roadmap & Post-Op Feeding
1
Lip Repair — ~3 months

Pre-op: optimize weight gain. Post-op: syringe/cup/spoon at corner of mouth; no pressure on lip repair; breastfeeding clearance ~2–4 weeks per surgeon.

2
Palate Repair — 9–18 months (varies by institution)

Pre-op: adequate weight, check iron. Post-op: liquid diet 1–2 weeks, soft foods weeks 2–4; no utensils in mouth; monitor for feeding refusal and oral aversion.

3
Alveolar Bone Graft — ~8–11 years

Pre-op: adequate nutrition for bone healing. Post-op: soft/liquid diet 4–6 weeks; high protein, calcium, vitamin D, zinc and vitamin C critical. No straws.

4
Orthognathic Surgery — late adolescence

Pre-op: proactive nutrition counseling, weight optimization essential. Post-op: liquid only 6–8+ weeks; average 5–10% body weight loss common; high-calorie supplements required.

★

Protocols vary significantly by institution and surgeon. Always coordinate post-op feeding instructions with your surgical team before counseling families.

Section 7
Syndromic & Complex Patients
Heightened nutritional complexity in Pierre Robin, Treacher Collins, 22q11.2 deletion, and other syndromes.
Section 7 · Syndromic Patients
Nutritional Reality & Approach by Syndrome

Pierre Robin Sequence

  • ~50–80% require NG in neonatal period
  • Prone/lateral positioning only — never supine
  • High aspiration risk with oral feeds
  • Airway management takes priority over oral feeding
  • Mandibular catch-up growth may restore oral feeding

Treacher Collins

  • Severe airway compromise — tracheostomy common
  • High G-tube rate; often tube-dependent long-term
  • Cleft palate in ~30%
  • Nutrition management needed across multiple surgeries
  • Condition-specific micronutrient monitoring

22q11.2 Deletion

  • Velopharyngeal insufficiency; nasal regurgitation
  • Hypotonia affects feeding coordination
  • Cardiac anomalies — increased energy needs
  • Hypoparathyroidism — monitor calcium closely
  • Condition-specific micronutrient deficiencies common

Principle for All Syndromic Patients

The more complex the syndrome, the earlier and more proactively dietitian involvement should begin — ideally at diagnosis, in the NICU or at the first multidisciplinary team visit. Reactive nutrition management in syndromic patients leads to worse surgical readiness and outcomes.

Summary
Key Takeaways: A Multidisciplinary Approach

Major Red Flags to Watch For

  • Feeds consistently >30–40 minutes
  • Poor or declining weight gain trajectory
  • Coughing, choking, or color changes during feeds
  • Recurrent respiratory illness
  • Caregiver unable to sustain feeding demands
  • Post-op feeding refusal or significant weight loss

When to Consult a Dietitian

  • At diagnosis — don't wait for weight loss to occur
  • Growth faltering or crossing percentile lines
  • Approaching surgical weight milestone
  • Pre- and post-operatively at every surgical stage
  • Any patient on enteral nutrition support

When to Consult SLP

  • Feeds >30–40 min or significant fatigue
  • Any respiratory symptoms during feeding
  • Suspected aspiration — consider MBSS
  • Post-op feeding refusal or oral aversion
  • Before initiating feed thickening
Thank You

Cleft and craniofacial patients face significant nutritional challenges from birth through adolescence. Early, proactive nutrition involvement is not optional — it is central to surgical success, wound healing, and long-term outcomes.
